Iran War Escalation Sends Oil to $96, Crushes Rate-Cut Hopes 
Oil's sitting at $96.06, up a modest 0.63%, but the real story is what's happening underneath. Shipping insurance premiums for Hormuz tankers are spiking as tensions escalate. LNG prices are up 60% year-to-date because of geopolitical risk. The Iran war is tightening global energy supplies, which normally would be bullish for gold as a safe haven. Except it's not. Here's why: the Fed just held rates and signaled only one cut this year, meaning the war's energy inflation is actually keeping the Fed more hawkish, not less. 
Energy price inflation is the one thing the Fed can't cut through. You can cut rates all you want, but if oil's $100, your real purchasing power still takes a hit. So instead of gold rallying as a war hedge (traditional playbook), we're seeing gold collapse because real interest rates are actually rising. The war is forcing the Fed to hold firm, and higher real rates kill non-yielding assets like gold, silver, and anything with a zero coupon. That's the contradiction that's crushing precious metals today. 
🔴 The Smoking Gun 
Hormuz shipping insurance premiums are spiking faster than headline oil prices can reflect. Historical data shows 50-75 basis point gaps between insurance costs and commodity futures, which means the market's anticipating a much tighter energy squeeze than the current $96 price signals. 

🟢 The Bigger Picture 
War-driven energy inflation is the Fed's worst-case scenario. It's stagflation-adjacent: growth headwinds from energy costs, inflation pressures that limit rate cuts. The Fed can't cut into energy inflation, so they hold firm. Higher real rates crush the entire risk asset complex. This is why gold's not rallying today despite geopolitical chaos. 

Fed Holds at 3.50-3.75%, Signals Only One 2026 Cut 
The Federal Reserve voted 11-1 to keep rates at 3.50-3.75%, and here's the kicker: they've cut their 2026 rate-cut projections from two cuts down to just one. The Fed's guidance is crystal clear: rates are staying elevated. GDP growth is holding at 2.4%, which isn't weak enough to justify emergency cuts. Inflation's at 2.7% in the Fed's projections, which is above their 2% target, so they're not panicking about deflation either. This is a "wait and see" Fed with a hawkish bias. 
That one dissent was likely a dove voting for a cut to address the Iran war's economic uncertainty. But the 11-1 vote shows consensus: hold. This hawkish hold rattled every asset class today. It killed rate-cut expectations that traders had been pricing in. It made non-yielding assets (gold, silver, crypto) less attractive because the opportunity cost of holding them just went up. And it signaled that if inflation stays sticky or energy prices spike further, the Fed's prepared to hold rates even higher for longer. 
🔵 What This Means For You 
Mortgage rates aren't dropping anytime soon. Risk assets get less attractive because the risk-free rate (Treasury yield) just got higher. Crypto becomes less of a "duration play" (betting on lower rates) and more of a "conviction play" (believing in the technology). If you were hoping for Fed cuts to boost your portfolio, adjust your timeline. One cut in 2026 means real rates stay elevated through year-end.

Gold CRASHES -5.66%, Silver PLUNGES -10.42% — Worst Since 2020 
Gold's at $4,619.20, down 5.66%. Silver's at $69.51, down a brutal 10.42%. This is the worst day for precious metals since the 2020 COVID crash. The move is counterintuitive because war usually bullish gold as a hedge. But the macro structure is broken today because the Fed's hawkish hold means real interest rates are rising, not falling. When real rates go up, non-yielding assets like gold become less attractive. You get better returns (safer returns) from Treasury bonds at 4.5%+ than you do holding gold that costs you 3.5% in real rate opportunity cost. 
Silver's getting hit even harder than gold because silver has an industrial demand component. It's used in solar panels, batteries, electronics. When the Fed signals higher rates for longer, growth expectations compress, which means less industrial demand. Silver's a barbell play: it's a monetary hedge like gold, but it's also an industrial commodity. When both thesis are broken (no rate cuts + slower growth), silver gets crushed worse. The day's range shows panic selling, not technical breakdown. That's actually a setup for recovery once fear normalizes.
