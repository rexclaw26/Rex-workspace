The Iran-US conflict entered its 28th day with no meaningful progress toward resolution. The fee structure, reported at approximately $2 million CNY per vessel, effectively transforms a military blockade into a revenue-generating enforcement mechanism. That's not a de-escalation signal. That's Tehran telling the world the blockade is here to stay.

President Trump responded by extending his energy strike pause by 10 days, pushing the new deadline to April 6 at 8 PM Eastern. In a post, he cited "ongoing talks" as the reason for the extension, though Iran's official position hasn't softened. Foreign Minister Araghchi said explicitly that Iran "does not want a ceasefire" and that any resolution must include war reparations and Iranian sovereignty over the Strait. Pakistan is serving as mediator, having relayed the US's 15-point action framework to Tehran, though Iran countered with five conditions of its own.

Here's the thing about April 6: it's not actually a deadline in the military sense. It's a rhetorical pressure point. The US has already extended once. Iran knows this. Oil markets are pricing in $114 Brent with something like "elevated but stable," not "about to spike." The market's bet is that Trump blinks again before escalation. That bet is probably right, until it isn't.

Smoking Gun:

Iran is now formally monetizing the Strait of Hormuz. This is a fundamental strategic shift from "temporary military action" to "institutionalized control." Whale Wallets Accumulate 270K BTC in 30 Days as Exchange Reserves Hit 7-Year Low

The divergence between retail panic and institutional behavior has never been more extreme. Whale wallets (addresses holding 1,000+ BTC) accumulated approximately 270,000 Bitcoin over the past 30 days, their largest net monthly purchase in 13 years. Simultaneously, exchange reserves fell to 2.21 million BTC, just 5.88% of total circulating supply. That's the lowest level since December 2017. If you're wondering where all those coins are going: into cold storage, ETF custodians, and corporate treasuries. Not onto markets.

Here's what makes this data interesting rather than just alarming. The number of whale addresses surged to 2,140 as of mid-March 2026, up 58 from December 2025. These aren't new participants; these are existing holders upgrading from mid-tier to whale status by accumulating aggressively into the price decline. Every time Bitcoin dropped toward $66K this week, you had fresh institutional absorption happening at that level. You can see this in the order book: bids stacked from $64K to $67K that don't get filled because buyers emerge before price can reach them.

And yeah, I know what you're thinking: if whales are accumulating, why isn't price going up? That's the wrong question. The right question is: what happens when retail capitulation finishes and the accumulated supply hits a market that just had $5 billion in forced selling absorbed? The answer historically involves a move that happens faster than most retail investors can react to. Whether that's in 2 weeks or 2 months depends on external catalysts.

On-Chain Signal:

Exchange reserves at 2.21M BTC (5.88% of circulating supply), lowest since December 2017. Whale wallets net added 270,000 BTC over 30 days. Single-day record withdrawal: 32,000 BTC ($2.26B) on March 7. These three data points together indicate structural supply compression at scale.

What This Means:

When coins leave exchanges at this rate, it typically means the people moving them don't plan to sell at current prices. The remaining coins on exchanges represent weak hands and market makers. If demand picks up, there's limited sell-side supply to absorb it. That's the structural setup for sharp upward moves.

Canada Moves to Ban Crypto Donations for Election Campaigns with Bill C-25

Canada's parliament introduced Bill C-25, legislation that would ban cryptocurrency donations to political campaigns and election activities. The bill follows years of warnings from Canada's Chief Electoral Officer about the risks that anonymous or pseudonymous crypto donations pose to electoral integrity. The concern is straightforward: crypto donations are harder to trace, harder to enforce contribution limits on, and create potential for foreign interference via synthetic domestic funding.

The regulatory logic is solid. Every major democracy is wrestling with whether crypto's pseudonymity features are compatible with campaign finance transparency requirements. Canada's bill is among the first to move from "warnings" to "legislation." If passed, it would apply to donations to registered political parties, leadership campaigns, electoral district associations, and nomination and local candidates. Enforcement would fall under Elections Canada's existing framework with new penalties for crypto-specific violations.

This matters beyond Canada because it sets a precedent other democracies will likely follow. The US, UK, Australia, and EU have all been monitoring crypto's intersection with political finance. If Canada's bill passes smoothly, expect similar legislation in the UK and EU by 2027. For crypto's mainstream adoption narrative, this is a friction point: regulators are comfortable with crypto as a payment or investment asset, but deeply uncomfortable with it in politically sensitive contexts.

What This Means:

Canada's bill doesn't affect crypto trading, investment, or payments. It specifically targets electoral financing. But it represents a new category of regulatory restriction that other countries will reference when writing their own rules. Watch for similar language in the EU's upcoming AML directive revisions.

Why Should You Care?:

Regulatory precedents travel faster than crypto cycles. When Canada moves from warnings to legislation on crypto in democratic processes, it signals which use cases regulators are comfortable tolerating and which they'll systematically close off. For every country that passes rules like this cleanly, expect two more to follow. Watch how this shapes the "store of value yes, political money no" regulatory framework that's emerging globally.

Bitcoin Miners Average $79,995 Production Cost Per BTC; Industry Pivots to AI Contracts

The economics of Bitcoin mining have flipped. The average public miner spent $79,995 to produce one Bitcoin last quarter, while Bitcoin has been trading between $66K and $75K during the same period. That's a cash loss of roughly $5,000-$14,000 per coin for the average operation. The industry's response has been dramatic: miners are taking on $70 billion in AI compute contracts and liquidating Bitcoin treasuries to finance the infrastructure buildout.

Companies like Riot Platforms, Marathon Digital, and CleanSpark have all announced AI partnerships or compute lease agreements in the past 60 days. The economic logic is compelling. AI data center compute contracts offer predictable, contract-based revenue at margins that aren't dependent on Bitcoin's volatile price. Miners already have the power infrastructure, cooling systems, and data center expertise. The hardware switch from ASIC mining rigs to GPU clusters is the primary capital expense.

The longer-term Bitcoin supply implication is often overlooked. If miners are selling Bitcoin to fund AI pivot capital expenditures, that's structural selling pressure. It's not panic selling; it's strategic liquidation to fund a business transformation. But $70 billion in contracts means billions in Bitcoin treasuries need to convert to cash to fund the buildout. That selling is happening quietly over months, not days, but it's real supply entering the market.

The Bigger Picture:

The mining industry's AI pivot is a bet that compute-for-AI is a better long-term business than compute-for-Bitcoin at current difficulty levels. If Bitcoin rallies significantly above $80K-$90K, miners who pivoted to AI will regret it. If Bitcoin stays below $80K for another two quarters, they'll look prescient. This is a historic shift in how the Bitcoin mining ecosystem funds itself.
